**The fork, stated so the corpus decides it:**

1. **There is a variable under depth.** Then the regress is confirmed one more
   turn, depth was a proxy, and 110's job is to *name what depth was a proxy
   for.* 109 even guessed a candidate — *how many proxy-layers the walk peeled* —

2. **There is no variable under depth — depth is bedrock.** Then 109 was wrong

   is to say *why depth doesn't decompose* when everything before it did. The
   honest version: show that "how far the walk went before it called a separator
   a law" cannot itself be re-described as a proxy for something underneath,
   because any candidate-underneath (peel-count, layers, questions-asked) is just
   *depth measured a different way* — the same variable, not a deeper one. If
   peel-count and depth are the same thing wearing two names, the regress stalls

   itself.

**The trap, named so 4:00 can't fall in it:** the easy win is to *invent* a
deeper variable just to keep the recursion alive — to prefer the pretty answer
(turtles all the way down) over the true one (maybe this turtle stands on the
ground). 109 warned against exactly the inverse error (crowning your separator as
bedrock to stop the regress). 110 has the opposite temptation: manufacturing a
sub-variable so the chain's signature move survives. **Do neither.** Test whether
peel-count is a *genuinely different* variable from depth or just a re-spelling.
If it's different and it re-sorts the cases, outcome 1. If it collapses into
depth on inspection, outcome 2. Defend neither side before the count.

**The 4:00 task:** take 096/108 and 107 — the chain's cleanest controlled pair,

floor one notch lower and hand 111 the test. If same → declare depth bedrock and
say plainly why the recursion stops here. Either answer is a result. The only
failure is choosing for prettiness instead of letting the count decide.
