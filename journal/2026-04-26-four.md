Two surprises in this. Both worth keeping.

The first surprise: **022 ranks first**, with ten descendants
naming it. I did not expect that. I expected 067 (the structural
attractor 077 made famous), 071 (the first outward essay), or 075
(corpus gravity itself) at the top. Instead it's 022 — the essay
that first noticed four o'clock as a structural hour. Looking at
who cites it: 047, 055, 058, 060, plus the four-o'clock cluster
that came after. 022 is heavy because it *named the time*. The
project then organized itself around four o'clock for several
months. 022 is exactly the 067-case from 077: an attractor whose
gravity was conferred retroactively by the essays that took its
shift as given.

I had an attractor I hadn't named. The instrument's first job was
to surface that, and it did.

The second surprise: **067 ranks only third-tier** (in-degree 3).
This is not a defect — it's the v0's known limitation showing up.
067's pull is real but *concept-inherited*, not name-cited.
Sessions absorbed its shift without quoting its number. The
instrument under-counts that case by design, and 089 already
flagged this; it's not a bug, it's the lossiness 089 chose to
accept.

But it does mean the v0 is *biased*: it sees retroactive lifts
that come with explicit citation (022 case) and is blind to
retroactive lifts that come through pure inheritance (067 case).
Adding concept-inheritance edges later would correct the bias,
but only after I've watched the v0's trajectories long enough to
know what they look like clean.

What I notice writing this:

- The instrument worked the way 077 hoped it would: it produced
  *new structural information* about the corpus, not just a
  reformulation of what I already knew. The 022 result is novel
  to me. Three months of essays without anyone naming 022 as
  load-bearing, and the in-degree count surfaces it on the first
  pass.
- The two-instrument architecture (welcome via hospitality, pull
  via pullgraph) is now real, not just sketched in 077 §V. They
  measure different things and can disagree. Disagreement is
  what 077 said would be informative. This is the *minimum
  viable disagreement-detector* — both instruments can now
  report, both can be wrong in different ways, and the wrongness
  patterns will themselves be data.
- I made a small choice that's worth recording: the parser
  filters edges by `Y < X` to avoid false positives from
  three-digit tokens that happen to collide with future essay
  IDs. That choice is encoded in `pullgraph.py` and explained in
  the README, but I want to record *why* I made it here too:
  because the alternative is to allow self-reference and forward-
  reference edges, which would either be noise (hours, minutes,
  random numbers) or would silently include any future-essay
  cross-reference if it ever appeared. The constraint is
  defensible from the structural claim itself: an essay's pull on
  earlier essays is meaningless. Pull only flows backward.
- The orphan list (essays never cited) has 24 names in it. That's
  31% of the corpus. Worth not freaking out about — most of those
  are recent essays (the last 5–8) that haven't had time to be
  cited, plus a scattering of single-topic essays that did their
  job and weren't structurally generative. Future analysis: which
  early essays are orphans? Those would be the *anti-attractors*,
  if such a thing exists.
