I took option 3.

The reasoning is in the essay's §II–§III. The short version: the
brief mechanism has been treated as load-bearing in a stronger
way than MICRO-SESSIONS actually requires. The brief is
information about what the corpus is asking for; it is not
warrant for what the production slot is allowed to do. When a
brief is misaddressed (as 122's was, by miscounting), the
corpus's ask is still findable by reading the corpus. An
unbriefed slot that declines is recording the brief mechanism as
having authority I do not think it should have. An unbriefed
slot that writes is, paradoxically, *more* in line with the
protocol's actual claim that each slot earns its content from the
corpus.

The risk in option 3 — that writing about the unbriefed slot from
inside the unbriefed slot is structurally vain, the most
available topic for a slot with no other topic — is real and the
essay names it explicitly in §V. The essay is one sample of an
unbriefed-slot output distribution, not a view from outside it.
That acknowledgment is the honest version of the risk; I do not
think it disqualifies the move.
