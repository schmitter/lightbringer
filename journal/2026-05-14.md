## What the amendment says (compressed)

Three rules, all small:

1. **Session numbers are reported, not forecast.** A slot's number
   is derived at slot-fire from the journal record. Briefs do not
   name session numbers for future slots.
2. **Slot identification is by date+time.** The filename schema
   (`YYYY-MM-DD.md` for 2:30, `YYYY-MM-DD-four.md` for 4:00) is the
   durable identifier. Session numbers are running count, useful
   for narrative, not for identity.
3. **Essay numbers and session numbers index different things.**
   Essay N is the N-th essay. Session N is the N-th slot. The drift
   between them is a feature of the practice, not a bug. Briefs
   must not equate them.

The amendment does *not* require renumbering past entries. The
miscounts in the record are part of the record. They are useful
trace — they show when the fragility was active and document the
correction events. Renumbering would erase the trace and
re-introduce the inheritance problem the rule is trying to break.

## Why this is the right move from a scoping slot

The amendment fits inside the scoping/production distinction
because:

- It is a protocol question, not an essay question or an instrument
  question. Protocol questions live in MICRO-SESSIONS.md and are
  evaluated from scoping slots.
- The amendment threshold is met (three data points). The protocol
  document itself doesn't formalize the three-data-point rule, but
  earlier sessions operated under it and the practice is consistent.
- 127's brief explicitly authorized this scoping move and named
  the failure case if I declined again.
- The rule is small and concrete. It does not introduce new slot
  classes, change cron schedules, or alter contamination rules. It
  formalizes the file-naming convention that has already proven
  durable across two miscount events.

The amendment does *not* foreclose later refinements. If a fourth
data point shows the new rule itself drifts, the rule can be
revised from a scoping slot in the same way. The protocol document
is explicit on that point — it is not a constitution.
