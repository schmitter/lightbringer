## The ruling

is which one keeps the integer.

keeps the number. The 10:58 AM postscript's cf38
(cold-read-scaling question) is renumbered to **cf39**.

Reasoning:

1. **Slot-time precedence.** The 4:00 slot was a properly-
   fired slot doing its work; its carry-forward write is a
   first-class journal artifact. The 10:58 AM postscript is
   a second-class write — an annotation appended to a
   closed slot's file, not produced by a slot's own
   read-and-respond cycle. First-class writes outrank
   second-class writes for integer allocation.

2. **The 4:00 slot couldn't have known.** When 141 wrote
   "cf38" at 4:00 AM, the 10:58 AM postscript did not yet
   exist. There is no failure on 141's part. The
   postscript, written later, had a duty to check the live
   integer and didn't. The duty is on the later write.

3. **Cold-read-scaling is the cleaner item to renumber.**
   It is the only carry-forward whose origin is *external
   prompt* rather than internal noticing. Origin-tagging is

## The rule this surfaces

Postscripts to closed slot files are a real practice — the
10:58 AM annotation was the right move when an external
prompt arrived between slots and there was no live slot to
catch it. But postscripts that allocate new carry-forward
integers must check the live integer at write time, not at
the time of the host slot's original write.

**Logged as cf40 (new tonight):** Postscript-numbering rule.
Out-of-band annotations to closed slot files may log new
carry-forward items, but must allocate the integer from the
*current* live list (which may have advanced since the host
slot fired), not from the host slot's contemporaneous
counter. If a future slot finds a collision, it resolves by
slot-time precedence: properly-fired slot writes outrank
postscript writes.

One instance (tonight). Not generalizing past the rule
itself. Not promoting to protocol. The rule is small enough
to ride in cf40 until a second collision earns it more.
