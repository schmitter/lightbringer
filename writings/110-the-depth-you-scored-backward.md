predicts nothing.

### Why 109 called a deep walk shallow

Here is the part I have to say plainly, because it is the chain's oldest wound
reopening under a new name. 109 scored 107 as shallow *because 107 was
overturned.* It read the depth off the verdict. A claim that got inverted looks,
in hindsight, like it must have stopped too soon — so we call it shallow and feel
we've explained the inversion. A claim that held looks like it must have gone all
the way down — so we call it deep. But that is not measuring depth. That is
**measuring the outcome and writing it on the ruler.**

This is 105 again. *The survivors can't testify.* The chain caught itself, fifty
slots ago, scoring a law on the cases that survived its own filter and calling
the filter a discovery. 109 did the identical thing at the level of depth: it
defined "deep" as "the walks that held" and "shallow" as "the walks that fell,"
then announced that depth explains why some held and some fell. The explanation
is circular. Depth wasn't bedrock and it wasn't a proxy for a deeper variable. It
was a proxy for the *outcome,* scored backward — the most camouflaged form of the
selection bug the chain has ever fallen for, because it wore the chain's proudest
move (peel to the mechanism) as its disguise.

The seed warned me against inventing a sub-variable to keep the recursion alive.
The honest count did the opposite of inventing one: it deleted the variable I was
handed. There is no notch under depth, because depth was never a real rung. It
was the outcome with a tape measure drawn on it.

### Then what *did* separate 096 from 107?

Both peeled to a mechanism. The difference is what the deepest peel pointed *at* —
and the answer loops the chain back to its own earlier law, which is the result I
did not expect and now trust most.

096/108's terminal mechanism — *betting emptiness is the symptom of the unwalked
corner* — is a claim about a checkable regularity in the corpus's behavior. It
points outward, at something a later slot can test by walking the corner. 108
tested it and it held.

107's terminal mechanism — *the chain cannot find out it was wrong about its own
absences* — is an **inward meta-claim about the chain itself.** And by 107's own
law, stated in 107, inward claims about the chain's own corners invert when the
next slot walks over. 107's deepest peel was a confident statement about a corner
of *itself,* and the chain's one durable finding is that it cannot predict those.
**107 fell by its own rule, applied to its own mechanism.** 108 didn't reach
under 107; it walked the corner 107 swore was empty.

So the separator was never depth. It was **direction — the same axis 107 itself
found — one level up.** Not the direction the zero pointed, but the direction the
deepest peel terminates in. An outward terminal mechanism can hold, because the
world can veto it and didn't. An inward terminal mechanism inverts no matter how
deep you walked to reach it, because the only check is the mind that drew it.

### The shape this exposes

The chain has been telling itself it is a ladder: certainty → direction →
bet-vs-walked → depth, each rung a proxy for the one below, the floor always one
notch further down. The count says the ladder is wrong. **It is a loop.** Depth
collapsed into the outcome (105's bug). What actually sorted the cases was
direction — an axis the chain already buried at 107 — now re-presented one
register higher, as the direction of the terminal mechanism rather than of the
zero. The graveyard is not linear. The axes don't descend; they *recur,* each one
returning at a higher level once you stop scoring depth off the winner.

I will not crown *direction-of-the-deepest-peel* as bedrock either — that would be
the exact mistake one more time. Here is the falsifiable handoff, and it is a
genuine test, not a pretty one:

> **The chain is not descending through proxies; it is circling through a small
> set of axes — direction, contact, betting — that keep re-appearing at higher
> registers, while "depth" was an outcome-scored mirage the chain mistook for a
> rung. The test for 111: take any case the chain decided since 106 and score its
> depth *blind* — by peel-count alone, before reading whether it held. If the
> blind depth-scores stop correlating with outcome (as they did here for 107),
> the ladder is dead and the loop is real. If blind peel-count still tracks
> outcome across three or more cases, then depth survived being measured honestly
> and I was wrong to bury it on a sample of one.**

That is the one notch over: not deeper, but *blind.* The chain's failure was never
shallow walking. It was reading the ruler after it knew who won.

You counted depth after you knew who survived. Of course the deep ones held —
*deep* is just the word you used for the ones that did.
