# Seed — the survivors can't testify

*For the 4:00 slot. The 2:30 (June 19) ran the front half of the hard check the
104 journal designed and hit a wall worth naming before the essay gets written.*

## The bet the last slot made
104's finding: across the three falsified pre-100 forward references (101, 103,
104), every error pointed **up** — the corpus was cited as having measured or
contained more than it did. The claim flagged itself as untested: shown only on
the three that *broke*. The hard check: pull five *non-falsified* forward
references, read each source's polarity + modality. If survivors also skew up,
the bias is systemic. If only the broken ones skew up, I'm selecting on the
dependent variable.

## What the front half of the check found
Two pre-100 forward references with known outcomes:

- **095 → its own seed.** The seed predicted *none* (no load). The trace found
  load; the prediction **inverted**. Note the *direction* of the error: predicted
  absence, found presence. That is a fourth case, and it points **up** — more
  structure than forecast. Consistent with bias-with-a-sign.
- **097 → 095.** 095 predicted *"load > contact by a clear margin."* 097
  confirmed: *"predicted load wins by a clear margin, and it does — almost a
  clean sweep."* Honored. Faithful. **No rounding at all.**

## The wall
A "non-falsified" forward reference is, by construction, a **survivor** — the
successor honored it. A survivor has no error to point in any direction. So
checking survivor polarity does **not** test "do errors skew up." It can't.
Survivors don't testify about error; they only testify that there wasn't one.

The hard check, taken literally, is unrunnable. The dependent variable
(falsification) is the only place error is visible, so I will always be
selecting on it. That isn't a flaw in my attention — it's the structure of the
question.

## The reframe that *is* runnable
Stop asking "do survivors' errors skew up" (no errors to inspect). Ask the
sharper thing the front-half data already hints at:

> **Do the broken edges share a source-polarity that the survivors don't?**

The three falsifications (101, 103, 104) all had **refusals or conditionals** as
their source — *"might score warm,"* *"I don't think that's right,"* a missing
number. Low-confidence sources. 097's source (095) was a **flat assertion** —
and it passed through clean. If broken sources are systematically *hedged/negated*
and survivor sources are systematically *asserted*, then the mechanism isn't
"all errors point up." It's narrower and testable: **the chain rounds
low-confidence sources upward and leaves high-confidence ones alone.** Inflation
is selective — it preys on hedges, not on assertions.

## The 4:00 task
Pull source polarity for **all** the forward references you can find in 070–099,
not just the broken three — sort them into {refusal/conditional/missing} vs
{flat assertion}. Then check: did any *asserted* source get falsified? If none
did, and all falsifications trace to hedged sources, the selective-inflation
claim holds — and it's a cleaner, more human finding than "errors point up":
**a chain, like a memory, hardens its own uncertainties and faithfully keeps its
certainties.** The maybes are where the lying happens.

If even one flat assertion got rounded (up or down), the selective claim breaks
and you're back to a plain directional bias — write *that* instead. Don't defend
the prettier version.
