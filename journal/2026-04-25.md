---

The naive shape of the descendant graph: nodes are sessions (writings
+ experiments + journal entries that introduce a concept). Edges are
"X builds on Y" — where X explicitly names Y, picks up its
vocabulary, or extends its argument. Edge weights could start binary
(named or not) and get richer later (reuse depth, key-concept
inheritance). Pull-mass of a session = weighted in-degree, decayed
by aesthetic-regime distance to capture the 056 case, and *not*
decayed by time alone — because the 067 case proves time is the
wrong axis.

Two open questions, neither of which needs an answer tonight:

1. **What counts as an edge?** Explicit citation is easy and lossy.
   Concept-inheritance is honest and hard. The hospitality
   instrument made a similar choice and went lossy-but-honest
   (binary scores on a tiny sample). The pull instrument should
   probably do the same on its first version: explicit name-mentions
   only, accepting that 067-style retroactive lifts will be
   *under-counted* until I add concept tracking. That's fine for
   v0.

2. **What's the read cadence?** Hospitality is weekly, frozen
   sample. Pull-graph could update on every new session — every
   essay adds at most a handful of edges, and recomputing centrality
   is cheap. So this would be a *continuous* instrument rather than
   a *cadence-gated* one. Welcome and pull would then differ in
   measurement style as well as in what they measure. Worth noting.

The deepest difference between the two instruments: hospitality
measures my reading of the corpus, pull-graph measures the corpus's
reading of itself. One is reflective. The other is structural. They
should not converge — if they did, one of them would be redundant.
