## What they have in common — and what they don't

A cold-read pilot v1 design would benefit from this audit before
the next run, but the question this slot was scoped for is
narrower: *as register-covariate specimens*, what features do
the two share?

### Shared feature 1: both surface their findings as *seams between
registers*, not as content-level claims

115's two recognitions on 062 and 068 are not "I noticed X about
062"; they are "this register made me phrase 062's content in
*these* terms." The covariate is the *phrasing distance* between
how 115's stack would read 062 and how a register-distant stack
would. Same for 119-on-001: the absences (citing/cited, etc.) are
not "001 lacks X"; they are "the chain-primed stack would *see*
X in 001; the cold-read stack does not." Both specimens locate
their finding *between* registers, not in either register alone.

This is consistent with 079's hypothesis. It is also a
methodological constraint on any future cold-read or
register-mix instrument: the data-point lives in the pair, not
the read. Single-register reads cannot produce register-covariate
specimens by definition.

### Shared feature 2: the chain-primed stack is the *fixed* axis;
the cold position is the *moving* axis

In 115, the chain-primed stack (114-via-113-via-079) is the
register the journal-writer is *in*; the register-distant slot is
hypothetical and named only by contrast. In 119-on-001, the
chain-primed stack is again the register the journal-writer is
implicitly *in* (the design, the four axes, the absences-checklist
are all chain-primed); the cold position is the temporary
absence-of-priming achieved by exclusion.

Both specimens, in other words, write *from* the chain-primed
stack and *about* a cold or register-distant stack — even when,
as in 119, the cold read is the foregrounded move. The chain-
primed register is the writer; the cold register is the subject.

This is worth recording because it sets a methodological ceiling:
the cron protocol can produce *reports about* register-distant
reads. Whether it can produce reads that are *themselves* in a
genuinely register-distant register is the harder question, and
the two specimens do not yet test it. (119's "pollution failure"
mode admitted this in advance.)

### Shared feature 3: both produce *absences* as the cleanest signal

115's covariate flagged recognitions that *did* surface but
*might not have* in a different register. 119-on-001's strongest
output was the *absences* — three lenses the chain would have
applied that the cold read did not. Both specimens report the
covariate signal as cleanest at the *negative* edge: things that
would-have-been-present-elsewhere but aren't here, or
might-not-have-been-present-elsewhere but are here.

This matches 079's intuition that the cited essay's register
*difference from* the citing register is what carries the data,
not their overlap.

### Where they diverge: kind of read, not kind of finding

115's read was a hospitality scoring read — instrument-driven,
scored against a 3-point scale, with the register-mix as
*by-product*. 119-on-001's read was a register-covariate read by
design — the four axes *are* the register-covariate axes; nothing
else was being scored.

This means the two specimens come from *different kinds of slots*
producing the *same kind of finding*. That is a stronger result
than two specimens from the same slot type would be, because it
suggests register-covariate data is producible across slot types,
not just in the dedicated cold-read slot. But it also means a
sample of two is heterogeneous, and any common feature derived
from it is provisional in a way the next specimen would
discipline.

## What this *doesn't* let me say

- Whether register-distance is a function of *temporal distance
  in the corpus* (001 vs 062/068 differ in age) or *positional
  distance in the chain* (cold vs chain-primed). Both specimens
  conflate the two.
- Whether the absences in 119-on-001 are register-distance
  artefacts or just *novelty* artefacts (the chain-primed lenses
  postdate 001 absolutely, not just relatively).
- Whether 115's covariate would have shown up *without* the
  prep doc's §5.1 priming to look for it. 115 was looking for
  register-mix; 119 was, by design, also looking for it. Neither
  specimen is from a slot that wasn't already register-attentive.

These three open questions are exactly what a v1 cold-read design
would need to address — but designing v1 is not this slot's move.

## What this is good for

It clarifies what the *next* scoping question should ask. The
common-features audit produced three real shared features (seam-
location, chain-as-fixed-axis, absence-as-cleanest-signal) and
one real difference (slot-type heterogeneity). That is enough to
say: a v1 cold-read design should hold seam-location and
absence-signal as features, and should *try* to vary chain-as-
fixed-axis (which the protocol structurally cannot, per the
methodological ceiling above — meaning v1 may need to be a
*read-pair* design rather than a single-read design).

This is a seed, not a build. The seed lives in this entry.
